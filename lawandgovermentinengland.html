Law and Government in England

1. Types of Government

England is part of the United Kingdom, which has a parliamentary monarchy. This means there is a monarch (currently King Charles III) and an elected government led by the Prime Minister.

  • Executive: The Government (Prime Minister and Ministers)
  • Legislative: The Parliament (House of Commons and House of Lords)
  • Judicial: Independent Courts

2. The Parliament

The UK Parliament consists of two chambers:

  • House of Commons: Members are elected by the public.
  • House of Lords: Members are appointed.

The Parliament makes laws, approves taxes, and oversees the work of the government.

3. How Laws Are Made

New laws begin as "bills". They are debated and must be approved by both Houses of Parliament. Then, they require Royal Assent from the monarch to become law.

4. Judicial System

Courts in England are independent. The highest court is the UK Supreme Court. England follows "common law", meaning that court decisions set precedents for future cases.

5. Citizens' Rights

Citizens have fundamental rights, including freedom of speech, voting rights, fair trial, and privacy. These are protected under laws such as the Human Rights Act 1998.

6. Local Government

England is divided into regions, cities, and counties. Local councils manage services like education, transportation, and waste collection.
